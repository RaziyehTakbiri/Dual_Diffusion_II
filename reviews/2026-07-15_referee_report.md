# Referee Report

**Manuscript:** Continuous-Time Dual-Manifold Diffusion for the Joint Generation of Coupled Discrete Structure and Continuous Expressive Attributes

**Review style:** NeurIPS/ICLR conference review; a journal (e.g., IEEE TASLP) framing is given at the end.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject (4/10).** The paper contains a genuinely interesting empirical nugget and is written with unusual honesty, but the headline claim is not yet protected against a trivial alternative explanation, the mechanism behind the CFC advantage is not identified, several promised results are missing from the manuscript, and the statistical support does not match the strength of the language. All of these are fixable; see the summary of required revisions at the end.

---

## Summary

The paper studies joint generation of a discrete structural stream and a continuous expressive stream ("manifold mismatch"), using symbolic piano performance (MAESTRO v3) as the benchmark. A beat-quantized score (88 pitches × 256 steps) is the discrete component; per-note velocity and signed micro-timing residuals (rubato) are the continuous component. The model runs an absorbing-state D3PM on the score and Gaussian/DDIM diffusion on the attributes under a shared diffusion-time index and a shared 12-layer diffusion transformer; a Gumbel-Softmax bridge (explicitly adopted from CoDi-style tabular work) couples the streams. The claimed contribution is the temporal model of the continuous stream: replacing the transformer's position-wise FFN with closed-form continuous-time (CFC) units. Across three seeds at matched capacity, CFC attains the best timing Wasserstein distance (0.0053 vs. 0.0120–0.0173), the best pitch-class Hellinger distance (0.167), and a rubato spread (σ≈22 ms) close to human (≈20 ms); a time-conditioned FFN and a Neural-ODE control localize the advantage to the *closed-form* continuous-time formulation. An AR baseline with a deterministic regression head collapses rubato (σ≈0.1 ms). A quantization sweep (16/8/4 bins) finds no systematic per-attribute gap. The paper explicitly reports that no backbone reproduces the temporal structure of rubato (generated |ρ₁|≤0.02 vs. human ρ₁≈0.12).

## Strengths

1. **Sharp, well-motivated question.** Isolating the temporal model of the continuous stream — rather than proposing yet another joint-diffusion architecture — is a good scientific framing, and the score/performance grid representation that preserves rubato as a genuine continuous signal (rather than sub-frame residue) is clean and reusable.
2. **Well-designed controls, as far as they go.** The time-conditioned FFN and Neural-ODE ablations rule out two natural competing explanations (the mere presence of a time input; continuous-time modelling in general). Most ablation studies do not think this carefully.
3. **Unusual scholarly honesty.** The authors explicitly disclaim novelty of the coupling mechanism, report a deliberate negative result (the quantization sweep), and foreground an unsolved limitation (rubato autocorrelation). This is commendable and should be preserved through revision.
4. **The magnitude-vs-structure distinction** (W₁/σ vs. lag-one autocorrelation) is exactly the right evaluation axis for expressive timing, and the regression-to-the-mean analysis of the AR baseline lucidly explains both directions of that comparison.
5. **Clear writing** and a clean factorized variational derivation (Eqs. 3–6).

## Major Weaknesses

**W1. The headline timing result is not protected against a trivial explanation.** By the paper's own measurement, generated rubato has near-zero lag-one autocorrelation. A deviation series with the correct marginal distribution and no serial correlation is, statistically, calibrated white noise around the grid. But matching the marginal is exactly what the two headline timing metrics (timing W₁ and |σ_G−σ_H|) reward — so a *trivial baseline that adds i.i.d. Gaussian jitter with σ=20 ms to a quantized score* would, by construction, achieve near-perfect timing W₁ and σ-match. Until this baseline is run and beaten — or the generated timing is shown to be conditionally structured (deviation as a function of metrical position, phrase position, chord asynchrony/melody lead, pitch register) — the central claim "CFC reproduces the magnitude of human expressive timing" is consistent with "CFC learned the right noise floor." This is the single most important revision. Note that the well-documented structure of human expressive timing (e.g., systematic lengthening at phrase boundaries, melody lead in chords) gives you concrete conditional metrics to test.

**W2. The mechanism behind the CFC advantage is not identified.** Equation (8) is a gated interpolation h(t)=σ(−f(x)Δt)⊙g(x)+(1−σ(−f(x)Δt))⊙h(0), and the manuscript never specifies (i) what Δt is in this instantiation (musical time between grid steps? diffusion-step interval?), (ii) whether Δt is constant, and (iii) what h(0) is inside a transformer block, i.e., whether the unit is applied position-wise or recurrently along the sequence. This matters enormously:

   - If Δt is constant on the uniform sixteenth-note grid, σ(−f(x)Δt) is just a learned input-dependent gate, and the CFC block reduces to a gated FFN (GLU/highway-style). The observed gain would then be attributable to *gating*, not to continuous-time dynamics — and the paper's carefully constructed narrative (closed-form continuous time is the decisive factor) would rest on an untested confound. **A gated-FFN control at matched parameters is required.**
   - If the unit is applied recurrently along the sequence axis, the comparison to a *position-wise* FFN confounds continuous-time dynamics with sequential state mixing; a plain RNN block (GRU/LSTM) control is then required.
   - The claim that the CFC "can place and interpolate events between grid points" is never tested. A direct test exists and would substantially strengthen the paper: train at one grid resolution and sample/evaluate at another (zero-shot resolution transfer). A genuinely continuous-time backbone should degrade gracefully; a grid FFN should not.

**W3. The autoregressive comparison is a strawman on the axis that matters.** The AR baseline predicts continuous attributes with a deterministic regression head, and the authors themselves note that "a distributional AR head could narrow the timing gap." Then it must be run: an AR model with a mixture-density or discretized-logistic head on timing/velocity is the fair comparison, since the current result conflates AR-vs-diffusion with deterministic-vs-distributional output. Separately, no comparison is made to any existing system, despite several being cited as motivation: SCHmUBERT (discrete diffusion with binned attributes — the direct competitor for the "bins sacrifice nuance" claim), Music Transformer / Performance RNN with time-shift vocabularies (which *do* emit expressive timing), or the expressive-performance-rendering line (see W9). At minimum, SCHmUBERT-style binned discrete diffusion on the authors' own representation should appear in Table 2.

**W4. Statistical support does not match the language.** Three seeds; Table 1 reports a ± only in the timing column (velocity and Hellinger appear as single numbers); Tables 2 and 3 report means with no dispersion at all. Section 5.2 promises Wilcoxon signed-rank tests or bootstrap CIs, but no test statistic, p-value, or CI appears anywhere; the abstract's "significantly better" and Section 6.6's "statistically indistinguishable" are therefore unsupported as reported. It is also unclear what a "per-sample" metric means for W₁, which is computed on pooled distributions. Finally, Section 5.2 refers to an evaluation set "of stated size" — the size is never stated — and no human-vs-human (held-out split vs. train split) noise floor is reported for any metric, without which the reader cannot judge whether, e.g., Hellinger 0.167 is close to or far from the achievable floor.

**W5. Promised results are missing, and the listening study is presented misleadingly.** Section 5.1 commits to inter-onset-interval W₁, groove consistency, Overlapping Area, MusPy descriptors, and the Fréchet Music Distance "for a headline comparison" — none of these numbers appear in the paper. More seriously, Section 5.3 describes a listening study in the present tense ("we conduct," "we recruit," "the protocol is pre-registered"), Section 6.8 states the objective results "are complemented by a subjective listening study," yet no subjective results exist in the manuscript, and the conclusion lists the listening study as future work. As written, the manuscript risks appearing to claim an unexecuted study. Either report the study or rewrite 5.3/6.8 unambiguously as a protocol proposal for future work. For a paper whose central claim concerns *perceptual* expressiveness, the absence of any listening results (and of an audio demo page — essential for music-generation papers) is itself a significant weakness: nothing here establishes that matching the rubato marginal is audible, and W1 suggests it may not be.

**W6. The validity of the representation — on which everything rests — is not established.** The beat grid is "estimated," with no method given. MAESTRO has no beat annotations, and beat tracking of expressive romantic piano is notoriously unreliable. If a single tempo is fit per piece, the residual r conflates slow tempo drift with note-level micro-timing, and residuals near half-grid will wrap to the wrong grid position. Notably, the reported human ρ₁≈0.12 is *surprisingly low* given the literature on expressive timing, where smooth tempo curves induce strong serial correlation; this suggests the grid itself may be absorbing or scrambling much of the temporal structure — in which case the paper's honest "no model captures rubato structure" limitation may partly be a *representation* artifact rather than a modelling failure, and the ρ₁ target itself is suspect. Also unspecified: how the deviation series is ordered for ρ₁ in polyphony (chord tones share onsets; melody-lead asynchrony is structured), how attributes are defined/losses masked at silent positions of the dense C∈R^{T×88×2} tensor (if the Gaussian objective runs over all T×88 positions, undefined entries dominate the loss), and a round-trip check (reconstruct performances from score+residuals; report fidelity) that would validate the encoding. Please report deviation autocorrelation as a function of lag and of metrical position for the *data*, not just models.

**W7. The quantization experiment does not test the hypothesis it cites.** The premise attributed to prior work concerns VQ-VAE *latent codebooks* — joint, learned compression of the sequence — whereas the sweep bins each attribute channel independently while keeping the same architecture and (apparently) the same Gaussian diffusion. Per-attribute binning at 8–16 levels is a much weaker intervention than latent vector quantization, so the null result cannot speak to the "quantization gap" of latent approaches (the authors' hedge "at the per-attribute level in our setting" concedes this, but the introduction's "we test this hypothesis directly" overreaches). It is also ambiguous whether quantized variants are retrained or post-hoc discretized, and Table 3 reports no variance, so the claimed indistinguishability and the non-monotonicity (b=16 worse than b=8) may both be seed noise.

**W8. Generality gap between framing and evidence.** The title, abstract, and Sections 1–2 pitch a general framework for "coupled discrete and continuous modalities," but all evidence comes from one dataset, one domain, one representation, and one grid resolution. For an ML venue, either add a second modality pair (e.g., handwriting strokes+characters, prosody+phonemes, or the tabular benchmarks of the cited baselines — where TabDDPM/CoDi/TabDiff could be run head-to-head) or reframe the paper as a symbolic-music contribution and target a music/audio venue.

**W9. Related work has significant gaps that affect the core positioning.** The claim that joint discrete–continuous models have "no notion of continuous time" is scoped to tabular methods, but the broader statement is contradicted by existing work the paper must engage: Campbell et al., ICML 2024 (Discrete Flow Models / multimodal flows, arXiv:2402.04997) jointly generate discrete sequences and continuous structure with CTMC-based *continuous-time* discrete flows — this is precisely a continuous-time joint discrete–continuous generative model, applied to protein co-design; see also continuous-time discrete diffusion (Campbell et al., NeurIPS 2022) and CDCD (Dieleman et al., 2022). Second, the expressive-performance-rendering literature solves exactly the score→(timing, velocity) problem and is entirely uncited: DExter (Zhang et al., 2024, arXiv:2406.14850) uses diffusion over continuous expression parameters conditioned on score — and independently reports that micro-timing is where models struggle, corroborating but predating this paper's limitation — alongside ScorePerformer, VirtuosoNet, and the basis-function models of Widmer et al. The novelty claim must be positioned against these. Finally, **references [3] and [4] could not be located** under their stated titles/authors/venues in a literature search; please verify or replace them.

**W10. Reproducibility and objective-consistency issues.** No code, no audio examples, and many unstated hyperparameters: γ, focal α_y/ρ, τ annealing schedule, β_t schedule, number of DDIM steps, Neural-ODE solver/tolerance/NFE (and wall-clock — CFC's efficiency advantage over NODE should be quantified), optimizer settings, and how "matched parameter count" was achieved. Two internal tensions should also be resolved: (i) Section 4.2 advertises "a valid joint variational objective... rather than summing two unrelated losses," but Section 4.5 then replaces the discrete term with a focal loss and weights the continuous term by γ — the trained objective is no longer the stated bound, so the rhetorical contrast with "two unrelated losses" should be softened; (ii) sampling uses activation-rate calibration to the training corpus — is an equivalent calibration granted to the AR baseline and all backbone variants? If not, structural comparisons are confounded by a test-time prior correction unique to the proposed model. Memorization is also unaddressed: distributional metrics are blind to copying, so an n-gram/excerpt-overlap novelty check against MAESTRO train is needed.

## Questions for the Authors

1. What exactly is Δt in Eq. (8) — musical inter-step time or diffusion-step interval — and is it constant across positions? What is h(0), and is the CFC unit applied position-wise or recurrently along the sequence? (W2)
2. Can you report the calibrated-jitter baseline (quantized score + i.i.d. N(0, σ_H²) residuals) in Tables 1–2, and conditional timing metrics (deviation vs. metrical position; chord asynchrony)? (W1)
3. Which significance tests were actually run, on what units, with what p-values? What is the evaluation-set size? (W4)
4. How is the beat grid estimated, and is tempo constant per piece? What is the round-trip reconstruction error of the representation, and what does the data's deviation autocorrelation look like beyond lag one? (W6)
5. Were the quantized variants of Section 6.6 retrained, or post-hoc discretized? (W7)
6. Was the listening study conducted or not? (W5)
7. Velocity is defined as v∈[0,1] in Section 3.2, yet velocity W₁ ≈ 0.3 — a third of the range — while the text calls velocity "modelled comparably." Are velocities actually in MIDI units [0,127]? Please state units for every metric. (W4/W6)
8. How are attributes at silent positions of C handled in the forward process and the loss? (W6)
9. Is the activation-rate calibration at sampling applied to the baselines as well? (W10)
10. The AR baseline is called "strong" — given its deterministic head produces σ≈0.1 ms of timing, on what axis is it strong, and can you add a distributional-head AR variant? (W3)

## Minor Comments

- Figure 2: the x-axis tick label appears corrupted ("iTme-cond."). Check rendering.
- Table 1 caption says "best in bold" and "mean ± standard deviation," but only the timing column carries a ±.
- "Approach.." / "Contributions.." — doubled periods from the paragraph macro.
- Eq. (8): note that the published CFC (Hasani et al., 2022) uses a third network h(x) in place of h(0); if this is a deliberate simplification, say so.
- Consider naming the model; "the CFC model" and "the framework" alternate throughout.
- The abstract's "high-dimensional benchmark" is fine, but "significantly" should be reserved for tested claims.
- Since Section 5.1 introduces FMD "for a headline comparison," the headline table should contain it.
- Consider reporting human anchor rows (train-vs-test human splits) in every table as the noise floor.

## Overall Assessment

- **Soundness: 2/4.** The controls that exist are good, but the central claims are exposed to an untested trivial baseline (W1) and an untested architectural confound (W2), and reported statistics do not support the significance language (W4).
- **Presentation: 3/4.** Clear and honest, but with protocol–results mismatches (W5) and missing specification of the core block (W2).
- **Contribution: 2/4.** The finding — closed-form continuous-time dynamics matter for expressive-timing fidelity, while time-conditioning does not — is interesting and, if it survives W1/W2, publishable; but it is currently a single-domain empirical observation built from adopted components, with positioning gaps (W9).
- **Rating: 4/10 (weak reject).** **Confidence: 4/5.**

**Journal framing (e.g., IEEE TASLP): major revision.** The required revisions are, in order of importance: (1) the calibrated-jitter baseline and conditional timing analyses; (2) the gated-FFN (and, if applicable, RNN) control plus full specification of Δt/h(0), ideally with the resolution-transfer experiment; (3) a distributional-head AR baseline and at least one prior-system comparison; (4) complete statistics (all cells with dispersion, actual tests, eval-set size, human noise floor); (5) resolution of the listening-study inconsistency and inclusion of the promised metrics, plus an audio demo; (6) representation validation (beat-grid method, round-trip, polyphony handling); (7) corrected related-work positioning against multimodal discrete–continuous flows and the performance-rendering literature, and verification of references [3]–[4]. None of these appears fatal to the core finding; a revision that survives (1) and (2) would be a substantially stronger paper.
